Thermal printers form images using heat on sensitive media or through a ribbon. Laser printers use electrophotographic imaging and toner, typically on cut sheets.

## Comparison

| Criterion | Thermal | Laser |
|---|---|---|
| Typical media | Rolls, receipt stock, labels | Cut-sheet paper, envelopes, some label sheets |
| Consumables | Direct thermal media or ribbon plus media | Toner and other model-dependent supplies |
| Color | Usually monochrome; specialized options exist | Monochrome or color models |
| Output shape | Continuous, cut, peel, or label workflows | Page-oriented output |
| Device footprint | Often compact | Commonly larger |
| Durability | Depends strongly on method and media | Suitable for many office records; environment matters |
| Common uses | POS receipts, shipping/product labels | Reports, invoices, contracts, office documents |

## Select by workload

For checkout, consider roll width, cutter duty, receipt lifetime, speed under real graphics, and drawer compatibility. For labels, test adhesive, facestock, ribbon, abrasion, chemicals, heat, sunlight, moisture, and scanner performance. For laser, test warm-up, duplexing, tray capacity, page coverage, label-sheet compatibility, and required finishing.

## Cost and reliability

Calculate cost per usable output using media, ribbons or toner, maintenance kits, printheads, wasted labels/pages, labor, downtime, and replacement cycles. Thermal printers have fewer image-forming consumables in direct-thermal use, but the media can cost more and may fade under adverse conditions. Laser devices consolidate office pages efficiently but can be unsuitable for roll-fed operational workflows.

## Decision checklist

- Is the output a receipt, individual label, or page document?
- How long must it remain readable, and in what environment?
- Are color, duplexing, cutting, peeling, or adhesive media required?
- What is the real daily peak and duty cycle?
- Can supplies and spare devices be stocked consistently?
